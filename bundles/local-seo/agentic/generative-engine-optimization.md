Generative Engine Optimization (GEO) is the practice of improving the visibility of source content in responses produced by generative engines. The term was formalised in the 2023 paper *GEO: Generative Engine Optimization*, which describes generative engines as systems that retrieve and synthesise information from multiple sources.

# Applying GEO to local search

The original GEO research evaluates content visibility in generated responses; it does not establish Google Maps ranking factors. For local search, GEO is therefore best treated as a data-quality discipline:

* publish clear, specific descriptions of the business, services and locations;
* keep names, prices, availability and policies consistent across the [Business Profile](/local-seo/gbp-signals.md), website and structured data;
* provide useful first-party evidence and cite authoritative sources;
* maintain descriptive, authentic media and current structured menus or catalogues; and
* make booking, ordering or contact paths explicit.

Google has not said that any one of these practices guarantees inclusion in [Ask Maps](/agentic/ask-maps.md).

# GEO and AEO

There is no single authoritative industry standard separating GEO from Answer Engine Optimization (AEO). In this bundle:

| Term | Working objective |
|---|---|
| AEO | Make a fact or answer easy for an answer system to retrieve and present directly. |
| GEO | Improve whether a source is used, represented or attributed in a synthesised generative response. |

The practices overlap. Both depend on accessible, accurate and well-structured source material.

# Media context

In April 2026, Google introduced Gemini-generated draft captions for contributor photos in Maps. The model analyses selected images and suggests captions that contributors can edit or remove. At launch, caption suggestions were available in English on iOS in the United States, with wider Android and international availability planned.

This update shows that Maps is adding machine-generated textual context to contributed media. It does not prove that captions are a ranking factor.
