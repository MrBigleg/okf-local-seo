Review gating is filtering customers by satisfaction before asking for a public review — typically a "how was your experience?" pre-survey that routes happy customers to Google and unhappy ones to a private feedback form. It sits in genuine tension with mainstream advice to "ask your happy customers for reviews", and since 2024 it carries regulatory as well as platform risk. The generation workflow it constrains is in [reviews on GBP](/gbp/reviews.md); the ranking dimension is in [reviews & reputation](/local-seo/reviews-reputation.md).

# Google's policy

Google Maps' prohibited-content policy bars merchants from conduct including, verbatim: "Discourage or prohibit negative reviews, or selectively solicit positive reviews from customers." The same policy prohibits offering incentives — payment, discounts, free goods or services — in exchange for posting, revising or removing a review, and prohibits pressuring users to review while on the premises. Merchants *may* "solicit or encourage the posting of content that does represent a genuine experience, without offering incentives". Enforcement ranges from content removal to feature limits and account suspension.

So the platform line is clear: ask everyone, incentivise no one.

# The FTC Consumer Reviews and Testimonials Rule

The FTC's Rule on the Use of Consumer Reviews and Testimonials (16 CFR Part 465) took effect on **21 October 2024** and authorises civil penalties for knowing violations — up to **$53,088 per violation** as of the FTC's December 2025 enforcement communications. Provisions relevant to review programmes:

* **§465.2** — fake or false reviews (including buying, selling or creating them).
* **§465.4** — compensation or incentives **conditioned on the review's sentiment**, positive or negative, express or implied. Incentives for reviews as such are not banned by this rule; incentives for *positive* reviews are (and remain banned by Google's stricter policy either way).
* **§465.5** — undisclosed insider reviews (owners, employees, relatives).
* **§465.7** — review suppression: false accusations against reviewers, or presenting a review set as complete while hiding negatives.

On gating specifically, the FTC's own Q&A is precise, and worth quoting: *"Can my business ask for reviews only from customers whom we think are happy with our services? The rule does not contain a specific prohibition against such conduct. But this practice could violate the FTC Act."* — citing the Endorsement Guides (16 CFR 255.2(d) and (e)(11)). Gating is thus not a per-violation penalty under the Rule itself, but it remains actionable deception under the FTC Act, and it always breaches Google policy.

The Q&A also marks the safe edges: making **generalised solicitations to all purchasers** is expressly exempt (even with an incentive offered equally to everyone — though Google still prohibits incentives); contacting negative reviewers to resolve their issue is fine; and simply asking satisfied customers to *update* an existing review is fine.

# Enforcement is active

* On **22 December 2025** FTC staff sent warning letters to **ten companies** flagging potential Rule violations — including review suppression and misuse of social-media indicators — reminding them of the $53,088-per-violation exposure. The letters were based on consumer complaints; they are warnings, not adjudications.
* The Rule was invoked in the May 2026 federal complaint over mass fake listings and fabricated reviews — see [spam & fake listings](/gbp/spam-and-fake-listings.md).

# Safe pattern vs risk pattern

| Safe | Risk |
|------|------|
| Ask **every** customer, through the same flow, at the same moment. | Pre-survey that routes only satisfied customers to Google. |
| Internal satisfaction surveys, run separately from review requests. | Survey that suppresses the review ask when the score is low. |
| Respond to negatives and resolve offline; invite an updated review afterwards. | Incentives to remove or revise a negative review. |
| Incentive-free requests via link or QR code. | Any incentive tied to sentiment; any incentive at all on Google. |
