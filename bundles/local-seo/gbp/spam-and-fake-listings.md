Fake listings are profiles for businesses that don't exist as presented: invented names, addresses the operator doesn't occupy, and phone numbers that route elsewhere. They work because local search rewards apparent proximity and apparent reputation — both of which can be fabricated at scale for far less than the cost of being a real local business. The victims are consumers, and equally the legitimate businesses whose [visibility](/local-seo/gbp-signals.md) they displace.

# The tactic set

Documented in the 2026 US federal complaint described below, and consistent with what [profile auditing](/maps/gbp-profile-audit.md) and [review intelligence](/maps/review-intelligence.md) see in the wild:

* **Fabricated local identities** — profile names invented per city, often keyword-loaded ("electrical services", "plumbing", "garage door repair") to match service searches.
* **Address abuse** — listed addresses belonging to unrelated third parties, or made up entirely; nobody is at the location.
* **Phone-number laundering** — local area codes that forward to centralised, often overseas, call centres.
* **Fake reviews at scale** — fabricated five-star reviews to bury genuine one-star reviews and lift the aggregate rating, sometimes using profile photos misappropriated from unrelated websites' staff pages.
* **Subcontracted fulfilment** — when a customer does book, the work is passed to third-party contractors posing as employees, with no control over qualifications or quality.

# Enforcement is no longer only Google's

Google's own enforcement runs through [verification](/gbp/verification.md), content policy and suspensions. What changed in 2025–26 is that listing fraud became a legal-enforcement matter:

* In **May 2026**, the US Department of Justice — acting on an FTC referral, with the Illinois Attorney General — filed a civil complaint against an Illinois company trading as "Premium Home Service" (B.E.S.T. GDR, LLC) and its CEO. The DOJ alleges the defendants created **over 15,000 fake business profiles on Google Search and Maps**, deceived **well over 100,000 consumers** since at least 2018, took **"tens of millions of dollars"**, and operated **over 7,600 phone numbers across more than 250 area codes** routing to overseas representatives. (Figures are allegations from the filing, not adjudicated findings.)
* The complaint invokes the FTC Act, the FTC's Consumer Reviews and Testimonials Rule (see [review gating](/gbp/review-gating.md)), the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act, and state consumer-protection law — a template other actions can reuse.

A widely reported "$79 million" gains figure for this case comes from investigative journalism, not the filing; the complaint itself says "tens of millions of dollars".

# Why legitimacy signals matter

The enforcement story is also the optimisation story in reverse. Everything a fake listing must fabricate is something a real business can prove:

* **Verification** — video and live-call [verification](/gbp/verification.md) exists precisely because postcards were spoofable at scale.
* **Consistent NAP** — a real address and phone number that match across the web ([NAP & citations](/local-seo/nap-citations.md)) is expensive to fake per listing.
* **Organic review patterns** — steady velocity from accounts with history ([review intelligence](/maps/review-intelligence.md)), versus the burst patterns fake operations produce.
* **A real web footprint** — [local schema](/local-seo/local-schema.md), a matching website, and photos of a premises that exists.

For a legitimate business, the practical defence against fake competitors is to report them through Google's redressal channels and to be conspicuously verifiable yourself; for the profile-integrity side of the same problem, see [profile shielding](/gbp/profile-shielding.md).
